Modulo arithmetic means we only keep the remainder after division by some modulus. In lattice cryptography, the modulus is usually written as $q$. Values are often stored, added, multiplied, rounded, compressed, and decoded modulo $q$.

Almost all applied cryptographers and software engineers already understand `%`. However, modulo plaats a special role in lattice cryptography and it's important to know how modulo values behave when they are used as noisy measurements.

# Modular arithmetic and wraparound

Modulo arithmetic means we work inside a fixed range. If the modulus is $q = 7$, then reducing an integer modulo $7$ gives one of these values:

$$
0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6
$$

This is pretty standard in the normal programming sense, any integer modulo $7$ becomes a number from $0 to 6$.

For example:

$$
8 \bmod 7 = 1 \\
15 \bmod 7 = 1 \\
-1 \bmod 7 = 6
$$

You read $8 \bmod 7 = 1$ as "when $8$ is reduced modulo $7$, the result is $1$".

This is the basic operation; take an integer, divide by the modulos, and keep the remainder.

# Wraparound

The reason modulo arithmetic behaves differently from ordinary integer arithmetic is that the range wraps around. With $q = 7$, the allowed values are:

$$
0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6
$$

After $6$, the next value is not $7$. It wraps back to $0$.

$$
6 + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod 7 \\
6 + 2 \equiv 1 \pmod 7
$$

This is the exactly what happens in lattice cryptography, values do not keep growing forever. They move around a fixed cycle.
