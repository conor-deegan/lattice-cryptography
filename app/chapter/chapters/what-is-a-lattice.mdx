Define a lattice as all integer combinations of basis vectors. Start with the normal 2D grid, then show skewed grids, then move to n dimensions. The key idea is that integer combinations give a discrete grid, while real combinations fill space.


# SOME MATHS HELP FOR 6 & 7

Read $\mathbb{Z}^2$ as "pairs of integers". The symbol $\mathbb{Z}$ means the integers, and the superscript $2$ means each point has two coordinates.

OR 

Read $\mathbb{Z}^2$ as "integer pairs": points $(x,y)$ where both $x$ and $y$ are integers.

Read $\mathbb{Z}^n$ as "integer vectors with $n$ coordinates". For example, $\mathbb{Z}^2$ has pairs like $(3,2)$, and $\mathbb{Z}^3$ has triples like $(3,2,5)$.

Read $z_i \in \mathbb{Z}$ as "$z_i$ is an integer". So $z_i$ can be negative, zero, or positive, but not fractional.

Read $\{(x,y):x,y\in\mathbb{Z}\}$ as "the set of all pairs $(x,y)$ where $x$ and $y$ are integers".

Read this as: "$L(B)$ is the set of every point you get by multiplying the basis matrix $B$ by an integer vector $z$".

The vertical bars just mean "put these vectors next to each other as columns". So $B=[b_1\mid b_2]$ is the matrix whose first column is $b_1$ and second column is $b_2$.

The $\sum$ symbol means "add these terms up". So $\sum_{i=1}^{n} z_i b_i$ means $z_1b_1+z_2b_2+\cdots+z_nb_n$.

# 6. What is a lattice?

A lattice is a discrete grid of points.

The simplest example is the normal 2D integer grid:

$$
\mathbb{Z}^2 = \{(x,y) : x,y \in \mathbb{Z}\}
$$

This is the set of all points whose coordinates are integers:

$$
(0,0), (1,0), (0,1), (2,3), (-4,7), \ldots
$$

You can draw it as dots on graph paper.

[Diagram: draw the standard 2D integer grid. Show dots at integer coordinates. Highlight $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, and $(0,1)$.]

This is a lattice.

But lattices do not have to be square. They can be stretched, slanted, or rotated. The defining idea is not the shape of the grid. The defining idea is how the points are generated.

## Start with basis vectors

Take two vectors:

$$
b_1 = (1,0)
$$

$$
b_2 = (0,1)
$$

These are the standard basis vectors for the 2D integer grid.

The vector $b_1$ points one unit to the right.

The vector $b_2$ points one unit up.

Now take integer combinations of these vectors:

$$
z_1b_1 + z_2b_2
$$

where $z_1$ and $z_2$ are integers.

For example:

$$
3b_1 + 2b_2 = 3(1,0) + 2(0,1)
$$

$$
= (3,0) + (0,2)
$$

$$
= (3,2)
$$

Another example:

$$
-1b_1 + 4b_2 = -1(1,0) + 4(0,1)
$$

$$
= (-1,0) + (0,4)
$$

$$
= (-1,4)
$$

The integers $z_1$ and $z_2$ tell us how many copies of each basis vector to use. A negative integer means we use the vector in the opposite direction.

With $b_1=(1,0)$ and $b_2=(0,1)$, all integer combinations give exactly $\mathbb{Z}^2$.

## Integer combinations are the important part

The word "integer" is doing real work.

If we only allow integer coefficients, we get separated points:

$$
(0,0), (1,0), (0,1), (3,2), (-1,4), \ldots
$$

If we allow real coefficients, we get the whole plane.

For example:

$$
0.5b_1 + 0.25b_2 = 0.5(1,0) + 0.25(0,1)
$$

$$
= (0.5,0.25)
$$

That point is not in $\mathbb{Z}^2$. It is between the grid points.

So:

$$
\text{integer combinations} \rightarrow \text{a discrete grid}
$$

$$
\text{real combinations} \rightarrow \text{a continuous plane}
$$

A lattice is built from integer combinations.

[Diagram: side by side. Left: dots from integer combinations. Right: shaded plane from real combinations.]

## A slanted lattice

Now choose different basis vectors:

$$
b_1 = (2,0)
$$

$$
b_2 = (1,1)
$$

Again, take all integer combinations:

$$
z_1b_1 + z_2b_2
$$

Some examples:

$$
0b_1 + 0b_2 = (0,0)
$$

$$
1b_1 + 0b_2 = (2,0)
$$

$$
0b_1 + 1b_2 = (1,1)
$$

$$
1b_1 + 1b_2 = (2,0) + (1,1) = (3,1)
$$

$$
2b_1 + 3b_2 = 2(2,0) + 3(1,1)
$$

$$
= (4,0) + (3,3)
$$

$$
= (7,3)
$$

These points form a slanted grid.

[Diagram: draw $b_1=(2,0)$ and $b_2=(1,1)$ from the origin. Draw several lattice points generated by integer combinations. Show the slanted grid.]

This is also a lattice.

It is not the standard integer grid. It is generated by a different basis.

## The 2D definition

In 2D, a lattice generated by two basis vectors $b_1$ and $b_2$ is:

$$
L = \{z_1b_1 + z_2b_2 : z_1,z_2 \in \mathbb{Z}\}
$$

Read this as:

> $L$ is the set of all points made by taking integer combinations of $b_1$ and $b_2$.

The notation is compact, but the idea is simple. Choose two integers. Multiply the basis vectors by those integers. Add the results. The point you get is in the lattice.

## The $n$-dimensional definition

In $n$ dimensions, we use $n$ basis vectors:

$$
b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_n
$$

A lattice point is:

$$
z_1b_1 + z_2b_2 + \cdots + z_nb_n
$$

where each $z_i$ is an integer.

So the lattice is:

$$
L = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} z_i b_i : z_i \in \mathbb{Z} \right\}
$$

This says the same thing as the 2D version, just with more basis vectors.

## Matrix notation

It is common to put the basis vectors into a matrix:

$$
B = [b_1 \mid b_2 \mid \cdots \mid b_n]
$$

The columns of $B$ are the basis vectors.

Then the integer coefficients are put into a vector:

$$
z =
\begin{pmatrix}
z_1 \\
z_2 \\
\vdots \\
z_n
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Multiplying $Bz$ gives:

$$
Bz = z_1b_1 + z_2b_2 + \cdots + z_nb_n
$$

So the lattice generated by $B$ is written:

$$
L(B) = \{Bz : z \in \mathbb{Z}^n\}
$$

This is the same definition.

It just uses matrix notation.

## Basis vectors must be independent

The basis vectors need to point in independent directions.

In 2D, this means one basis vector must not be a scalar multiple of the other.

These two vectors are independent:

$$
(1,0), \quad (0,1)
$$

These are not:

$$
(1,0), \quad (2,0)
$$

The second vector is just twice the first. Both lie on the same line.

Integer combinations of $(1,0)$ and $(2,0)$ only produce points on the x-axis:

$$
z_1(1,0) + z_2(2,0) = (z_1 + 2z_2, 0)
$$

The second coordinate is always zero.

So these vectors do not generate a 2D lattice in the plane. They generate a 1D lattice sitting inside 2D space.

[Diagram: draw two collinear vectors on the x-axis. Show that their integer combinations only produce points on a line.]

For this series, when we say an $n$-dimensional lattice, we usually mean a full-rank lattice in $n$-dimensional space. That means it has $n$ independent basis vectors.

## Notation table

| Symbol | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | The integers | $\ldots,-2,-1,0,1,2,\ldots$ |
| $\mathbb{Z}^2$ | Pairs of integers | $(3,2)$, $(-1,4)$ |
| $b_i$ | A basis vector | $b_1=(1,0)$ |
| $z_i$ | An integer coefficient | $z_1=3$ |
| $z$ | A vector of integer coefficients | $z=(3,2)$ |
| $B$ | A matrix whose columns are basis vectors | $B=[b_1 \mid b_2]$ |
| $Bz$ | An integer combination of the basis vectors | $3b_1+2b_2$ |
| $L(B)$ | The lattice generated by $B$ | all points $Bz$ for $z\in\mathbb{Z}^n$ |

## What to remember

A lattice is a discrete grid of points generated by integer combinations of basis vectors.

In 2D:

$$
L = \{z_1b_1 + z_2b_2 : z_1,z_2 \in \mathbb{Z}\}
$$

In $n$ dimensions:

$$
L(B) = \{Bz : z \in \mathbb{Z}^n\}
$$

Integer coefficients make the lattice discrete.

Real coefficients would fill the surrounding space.

A basis is a compact description of an infinite set of lattice points.

## What can stay fuzzy for now

You do not need to know which lattice problems are hard yet.

You do not need determinants, dual lattices, shortest vectors, or attacks yet.

For now, the main idea is:

> A lattice is all integer combinations of its basis vectors.
