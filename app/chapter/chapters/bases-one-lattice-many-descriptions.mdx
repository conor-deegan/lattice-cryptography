# 7. Bases: one lattice, many descriptions

A basis is a description of a lattice.

It is not the lattice itself.

The same lattice can have more than one basis. This is one of the first ideas that makes lattices different from the usual way we think about grids.

## The standard basis for $\mathbb{Z}^2$

The usual integer grid is:

$$
\mathbb{Z}^2
$$

The most natural basis is:

$$
e_1 = (1,0)
$$

$$
e_2 = (0,1)
$$

Every point in $\mathbb{Z}^2$ can be written as:

$$
z_1e_1 + z_2e_2
$$

For example:

$$
3e_1 + 2e_2 = 3(1,0) + 2(0,1)
$$

$$
= (3,2)
$$

So the basis $(e_1,e_2)$ generates $\mathbb{Z}^2$.

[Diagram: draw $\mathbb{Z}^2$. Show $e_1=(1,0)$ and $e_2=(0,1)$.]

## A different basis for the same lattice

Now take these vectors:

$$
b_1 = (1,1)
$$

$$
b_2 = (0,1)
$$

These vectors look different from the standard basis.

One points diagonally.

One points up.

But they also generate $\mathbb{Z}^2$.

[Diagram: draw the same $\mathbb{Z}^2$ grid. Show $b_1=(1,1)$ and $b_2=(0,1)$. The grid points should be the same dots as before.]

To see why, we need to check whether these vectors can generate the standard basis vectors $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$.

The vector $(0,1)$ is already there:

$$
b_2 = (0,1)
$$

For $(1,0)$, use this integer combination:

$$
b_1 - b_2
$$

This means:

$$
1\cdot b_1 + (-1)\cdot b_2
$$

The coefficient $-1$ is allowed because lattice combinations use integers, and $-1$ is an integer.

Now compute it:

$$
b_1 - b_2 = (1,1) - (0,1)
$$

$$
= (1,0)
$$

So the basis $(b_1,b_2)$ can generate both:

$$
(1,0)
$$

and:

$$
(0,1)
$$

Once it can generate those two vectors, it can generate every point in $\mathbb{Z}^2$.

So:

$$
L(b_1,b_2) = \mathbb{Z}^2
$$

even though $b_1$ and $b_2$ are not the standard basis.

## Why subtraction is allowed

Subtraction is not a special operation outside the lattice definition.

It is just an integer combination.

$$
b_1 - b_2
$$

means:

$$
1\cdot b_1 + (-1)\cdot b_2
$$

The lattice contains every integer combination of the basis vectors. That includes positive multiples, zero multiples, and negative multiples.

So if $b_1$ and $b_2$ are basis vectors, then:

$$
b_1 + b_2
$$

is a lattice vector, and:

$$
b_1 - b_2
$$

is also a lattice vector.

For example, with:

$$
b_1=(1,1), \quad b_2=(0,1)
$$

we get:

$$
b_1 + b_2 = (1,2)
$$

and:

$$
b_1 - b_2 = (1,0)
$$

Both are lattice vectors.

The reason we used subtraction above is simple: it was the integer combination that produced $(1,0)$. Addition would produce a different lattice vector.

## Same points, different coordinates

A basis gives coordinates for lattice points.

With the standard basis:

$$
e_1=(1,0), \quad e_2=(0,1)
$$

the point $(3,2)$ has coordinates:

$$
(3,2)
$$

because:

$$
(3,2)=3e_1+2e_2
$$

With the basis:

$$
b_1=(1,1), \quad b_2=(0,1)
$$

the same point has different coordinates.

We want integers $z_1,z_2$ such that:

$$
z_1b_1+z_2b_2=(3,2)
$$

That is:

$$
z_1(1,1)+z_2(0,1)=(3,2)
$$

The first coordinate is $z_1$, so:

$$
z_1=3
$$

The second coordinate is $z_1+z_2$, so:

$$
3+z_2=2
$$

which gives:

$$
z_2=-1
$$

So:

$$
(3,2)=3b_1-b_2
$$

The point is the same.

The coordinates are different because the basis is different.

This is the core idea:

> The lattice is the set of points. The basis is a coordinate system for those points.

## A second example

Take:

$$
c_1 = (1,0)
$$

$$
c_2 = (1,1)
$$

These also generate $\mathbb{Z}^2$.

The vector $(1,0)$ is already:

$$
c_1
$$

The vector $(0,1)$ is:

$$
c_2 - c_1
$$

because:

$$
(1,1)-(1,0)=(0,1)
$$

So $(c_1,c_2)$ generates the same lattice as the standard basis.

Again, subtraction is not the point. The point is that integer combinations of $c_1$ and $c_2$ can produce the standard basis vectors.

[Diagram: draw $\mathbb{Z}^2$. Show $c_1=(1,0)$ and $c_2=(1,1)$. Then show $c_2-c_1=(0,1)$.]

## How to show two bases generate the same lattice

For now, the cleanest way is this:

To show two bases generate the same lattice, show that each basis can be built from integer combinations of the other.

For example, compare:

$$
E = \{e_1=(1,0), e_2=(0,1)\}
$$

and:

$$
B = \{b_1=(1,1), b_2=(0,1)\}
$$

First, build the vectors of $B$ using $E$:

$$
b_1 = e_1 + e_2
$$

$$
b_2 = e_2
$$

So everything generated by $B$ is also generated by $E$.

Now build the vectors of $E$ using $B$:

$$
e_1 = b_1 - b_2
$$

$$
e_2 = b_2
$$

So everything generated by $E$ is also generated by $B$.

Therefore both bases generate the same lattice.

$$
L(E)=L(B)=\mathbb{Z}^2
$$

This is the only test we need at this point.

## Matrix version

Let:

$$
E =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
$$

and:

$$
B =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
$$

The columns of $B$ are:

$$
b_1=(1,1), \quad b_2=(0,1)
$$

The lattice generated by $B$ is:

$$
L(B)=\{Bz:z\in\mathbb{Z}^2\}
$$

Take:

$$
z =
\begin{pmatrix}
3 \\
-1
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Then:

$$
Bz =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
3 \\
-1
\end{pmatrix}
$$

$$
=
\begin{pmatrix}
3 \\
2
\end{pmatrix}
$$

So the coefficient vector $(3,-1)$ under basis $B$ represents the lattice point $(3,2)$.

Under the standard basis, the same lattice point has coefficient vector $(3,2)$.

Same point.

Different basis.

Different coordinates.

## Why this matters

The basis affects how the lattice looks to us.

For the standard basis, $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is easy to read.

For the basis:

$$
(1,1), (0,1)
$$

it is still easy, but slightly less direct.

Later we will see bases that describe the same lattice in much less convenient ways.

That will matter because many lattice problems are easy or hard depending on the description you are given.

For this chapter, we only need the first step:

> A lattice can have many bases.

## Notation table

| Symbol | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| $E$ | The standard basis matrix | columns $(1,0),(0,1)$ |
| $B$ | Another basis matrix | columns $(1,1),(0,1)$ |
| $L(B)$ | The lattice generated by $B$ | all $Bz$ for integer $z$ |
| $z$ | Coordinates relative to a basis | $(3,-1)$ under $B$ |
| $Bz$ | The lattice point represented by $z$ under $B$ | $(3,2)$ |
| $b_1-b_2$ | An integer combination | $1b_1+(-1)b_2$ |

## What to remember

A basis is a description of a lattice.

The same lattice can have more than one basis.

A lattice point can have different coordinates under different bases.

Subtraction is allowed because lattice combinations use integer coefficients, including negative integers.

To show two bases generate the same lattice, show that each basis can be built from integer combinations of the other.

## What can stay fuzzy for now

You do not need determinants.

You do not need unimodular matrices yet.

You do not need lattice reduction yet.

The only idea to keep is:

> The lattice is the set of points. The basis is one way to describe those points.
