Lattices for Applied Cryptographers

Part 1: The tools you need before lattices

1. Why lattice cryptography feels strange at first

Set the reader’s expectations. Lattice crypto does not start from groups and scalar multiplication like ECC. It starts from high-dimensional grids, short vectors, noisy equations, and modular linear algebra. The goal of this chapter is to give the reader the map before they start walking.

2. Vectors, length, and “short” things

Introduce vectors as lists of numbers. Explain vector length, Euclidean norm, coefficient size, and why cryptographers keep saying “short”. This chapter should make ||x|| feel like a practical measurement, not abstract notation.

3. Dot products, matrices, and many equations at once

Explain dot products first, then matrix-vector multiplication as a batch of dot products. This prepares the reader for equations like As, Az, and <a, s>.

4. Modular arithmetic and wraparound

Explain arithmetic modulo q, centered representatives, wraparound, and why values “near 0” or “near q/2” matter. This is the foundation for understanding rounding, decryption, compression, and failures.

5. Sampling, randomness, and noise

Explain uniform sampling, small secrets, small errors, binomial distributions, and the idea of sampling from a distribution. Do not introduce full Gaussian machinery yet. Just make the reader comfortable with “choose a small random vector”.

⸻

Part 2: Lattices from first principles

6. What is a lattice?

Define a lattice as all integer combinations of basis vectors. Start with the normal 2D grid, then show skewed grids, then move to n dimensions. The key idea is that integer combinations give a discrete grid, while real combinations fill space.

7. Bases: one lattice, many descriptions

Explain that a basis is a description of a lattice, not the lattice itself. Show that the same lattice can have many different bases. This is the first major “aha” moment.

8. Good bases and bad bases

Explain why some bases make the lattice easy to work with and others make it hard. Use 2D nearest-point examples. This chapter introduces the basic trapdoor intuition: same lattice, different descriptions, different difficulty.

9. Distance, shortest vectors, and minimum distance

Introduce the shortest nonzero lattice vector and λ1. Explain why the minimum distance tells us how close lattice points are to each other. This prepares the reader for both attacks and correctness.

10. Closest points and decoding

Introduce the closest vector problem informally. Show that if a point is a lattice point plus small noise, decoding means finding the original lattice point. This becomes the mental model for decryption.

11. Fundamental regions: reducing modulo a lattice

Explain the fundamental parallelepiped as the lattice version of modular reduction. This chapter connects the earlier “mod q” intuition to full lattice geometry.

12. The dual lattice, gently

Introduce the dual lattice only after the reader understands lattices, bases, and dot products. Explain it as the set of vectors that pair cleanly with the original lattice. Keep the focus on why it later appears in reductions, smoothing, and LWE.

⸻

Part 3: The hard problems

13. SVP: finding the shortest vector

Define the shortest vector problem. Explain why it is easy to see in 2D but hard in high dimension when the basis is bad. This chapter gives the first formal hardness problem.

14. Approximate SVP and why approximation matters

Explain approximation factors. Make clear that crypto usually does not rely on exact SVP, but on finding vectors within some factor of optimal. This is needed before discussing worst-case reductions honestly.

15. CVP and BDD: finding the nearest lattice point

Define closest vector problem and bounded distance decoding. Connect BDD directly to the decryption story: recover the original point from a nearby noisy point.

16. Why high-dimensional lattices are hard

Explain why brute force, drawing pictures, and intuition from 2D fail in high dimension. Introduce the idea of lattice reduction as the main attack direction, but do not go deep yet.

⸻

Part 4: q-ary lattices, the bridge into real crypto

17. Lattices defined by modular equations

Introduce q-ary lattices. Show that equations like Az = 0 mod q define a lattice inside the integers. This is the bridge from geometric lattices to the modular equations used in schemes.

18. Public matrices as lattice descriptions

Explain how a public random matrix can define a hard lattice problem. This chapter makes it natural that a public key might be a matrix, or a seed that expands into a matrix.

⸻

Part 5: SIS, the short relation problem

19. SIS from scratch

Define SIS in plain English: given a public matrix A, find a short nonzero vector z such that Az = 0 mod q. Use small numeric examples before notation.

20. Why SIS is hard

Explain why Az = 0 mod q is easy without the shortness condition. Then show why the shortness bound is exactly what turns it into a lattice problem. Also explain trivial solutions like z = (q,0,...,0) and why parameters rule them out.

21. SIS as hashing, commitments, and signatures

Show the function f_A(x)=Ax mod q. Explain why a collision gives a short SIS solution. Then connect this to commitments and the verification equations that appear in lattice signatures.

⸻

Part 6: LWE, the noisy equation problem

22. LWE from scratch

Start with exact linear equations and show that they are easy. Then add small errors and show that the system becomes hard to solve. Introduce the core equation: b = <a, s> + e mod q.

23. Search-LWE and Decision-LWE

Explain the difference between recovering the secret and distinguishing noisy equations from random. This is important because encryption security usually relies on pseudorandomness, not just recovery hardness.

24. LWE encryption by hand

Build a toy LWE encryption scheme step by step. Show key generation, encryption, and decryption. Expand the cancellation equation carefully until the reader sees why the secret key recovers the message.

25. Noise, rounding, and correctness failure

Explain how messages are encoded into regions and recovered by rounding. Show exactly what happens when noise gets too large. This chapter should make decryption failures feel concrete.

26. Worst-case to average-case hardness

Explain the big theorem idea for SIS and LWE: solving random crypto instances would imply solving hard lattice problems in the worst case. Be honest about approximation factors, parameters, and the fact that this is not the same thing as a proof that deployed parameters are unbreakable.

⸻

Part 7: Rings and modules, the move from clean theory to practical schemes

27. Why plain SIS and LWE are too large

Show why unstructured matrices are expensive. This motivates structure from first principles, rather than dropping polynomial rings on the reader out of nowhere.

28. Polynomials as compressed vectors

Introduce polynomials as vectors of coefficients. Explain rings like Z_q[x]/(x^n + 1) using small examples. The goal is to make polynomial arithmetic feel like structured vector arithmetic.

29. Polynomial multiplication as structured matrix multiplication

Show that multiplying by a polynomial is equivalent to multiplying by a special structured matrix. This is the core efficiency idea behind ring and module lattice crypto.

30. Ring-LWE, Ring-SIS, and module lattices

Now introduce Ring-LWE and Ring-SIS as the same SIS/LWE ideas over polynomial rings. Then introduce module lattices as the middle ground between plain matrices and pure ring structure. This chapter directly prepares the reader for ML-KEM and ML-DSA.

⸻

Part 8: ML-KEM

31. From encryption to KEMs

Explain what a KEM is: key generation, encapsulation, decapsulation, ciphertext, shared secret. Explain why modern protocols usually use KEMs rather than raw public-key encryption.

32. ML-KEM under the hood

Map ML-KEM to the ideas already built:

Module-LWE style equations
small secrets and errors
polynomial/module arithmetic
compression
rounding
CCA transform
decapsulation checks

This chapter should not be a line-by-line standard walkthrough. It should explain why every moving part exists.

⸻

Part 9: ML-DSA and lattice signatures

33. What a lattice signature proves

Explain that lattice signatures usually prove knowledge of short secret values satisfying public modular relations. This chapter reconnects signatures to SIS.

34. Fiat-Shamir signatures from lattice relations

Explain the commit-challenge-response structure. Then explain Fiat-Shamir: the challenge becomes a hash of the message and commitment.

35. Rejection sampling and leakage

Explain why signature responses can leak information about the secret. Then explain rejection sampling as a way to shape the output distribution so signatures are safe.

36. ML-DSA under the hood

Map ML-DSA to the ideas already built:

Module-SIS style verification
short secrets
commitment
challenge hash
response
norm bounds
rejection sampling

The reader should leave understanding the shape of the scheme, not just the API.

⸻

Part 10: Falcon, NTRU, and the other lattice signature path

37. NTRU intuition

Explain NTRU as hiding a relationship between short polynomials. Introduce the mental model: the public key hides a ratio of short secrets, and the secret key lets you undo noisy products.

38. NTRU lattices

Show how NTRU creates a structured lattice containing unusually short secret vectors. This connects NTRU back to the geometry from earlier chapters.

39. Hash-and-sign and trapdoor sampling

Explain the Falcon-style path: hash the message to a target, then use a trapdoor to find a short preimage. This is different from the Fiat-Shamir path used by ML-DSA.

40. Gaussian sampling and Falcon under the hood

Explain why Falcon signatures are small and why the implementation is hard. Cover Gaussian sampling, NTRU trapdoors, and the fact that safe signing must avoid leaking the secret basis.

⸻

Part 11: Attacks, parameters, and implementation

41. How lattice attacks work at a high level

Introduce primal attacks, dual attacks, and hybrid attacks. Keep the focus on intuition: attackers try to turn scheme-breaking into finding short vectors or distinguishing noisy equations.

42. Lattice reduction and security estimates

Explain LLL, BKZ, sieving, and enumeration at a high level. Explain that practical security estimates are based on the cost of the best known attacks, not on exact proofs of concrete parameters.

43. Parameters and tradeoffs

Explain how dimension, module rank, modulus, noise width, compression, and norm bounds affect security, correctness, size, and speed. This chapter ties the whole series together.

44. Implementation failure modes

Cover constant-time arithmetic, sampling bugs, malformed inputs, serialization, domain separation, decapsulation failures, rejection sampling leakage, and side channels. This is the applied cryptographer’s checklist.

45. PQC migration and scheme selection

End with deployment. Explain where ML-KEM, ML-DSA, Falcon, NTRU-style systems, and hash-based signatures fit. Cover hybrid migration, certificates, hardware constraints, and crypto-agility.

⸻

Final recommendation

For the first web version, write 45 chapters.

That is the right size.

Fewer than this and you will start hiding complexity. More than this and it will feel like an encyclopedia before anyone starts reading.
